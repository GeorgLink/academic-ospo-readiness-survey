# Communications plan

A simple plan for running the survey as a short campaign. Adjust the timing to your institution's calendar. The ready-to-edit messages are in [22-invitation-templates.md](22-invitation-templates.md).

## The shape of the campaign

A typical survey duration is about three weeks: a short warm-up, a launch, two reminders, and a close. A campaign with reminders typically collects far more responses than a single email.

| When | Step | What you send | To whom |
|------|------|---------------|---------|
| Week 0 (a few days before) | Warm-up *(optional)* | A one-line heads-up that the survey is coming, ideally from a leader | Everyone, or your target groups |
| Week 1, day 1 | Launch | The invitation, with the link | Everyone / your participant list |
| Week 2 | Reminder 1 | A short nudge | Non-responders (or everyone, if using an open link) |
| Week 3, two days before close | Reminder 2 (final) | "Closing soon" nudge | Non-responders |
| Week 3, end | Close | Survey closes; thank-you note *(optional)* | Everyone |
| After analysis | Share back | A short summary of what you heard and what you'll do | Everyone, especially anyone who emailed the survey contact to hear the outcome |

## Running it small (no sponsor)

You do not need an institution-wide campaign or a senior endorsement to get useful results. If you are running the survey grassroots, among your own group, team, or network of colleagues, scale the plan down.

- Send a short personal note to people you know, rather than a broadcast. A direct "I'm trying to understand what open source support we need here; would you take fifteen minutes?" gets a better response than a formal announcement.
- Skip the sponsor and endorsement steps. They help a large campaign, but they are not required.
- Use an open survey link so anyone you share it with can respond.
- A dozen thoughtful responses from the people closest to the work is a good starting point. You can widen distribution later if the results warrant it.

Share the results back to the people who took part, and use them to make the case for more support, or for an OSPO. The rest of this plan still applies; you are working at a smaller scale.

## Audiences and who should send

- **The invitation** lands best from a named, recognisable person, not a no-reply address. A research-software lead, library director, or IT leader works well.
- **An endorsement** from a dean, CIO, or pro-vice-chancellor (even a single sentence) raises response rates more than anything else. Ask for one and include it in, or alongside, the invitation.
- **Reminders** can come from the same sender as the invitation.

## Channels

Email is the backbone. Reinforce it where your community already gathers:

- A message in relevant Slack/Teams/Mattermost channels (template provided).
- A line in an existing newsletter or staff bulletin.
- A mention at a regular meeting, seminar, or community call.
- For students, your usual student channels and any relevant course or society lists.

Don't rely on a single channel. A combination of one email plus one community post usually beats two emails.

## Reminder cadence

- Two reminders, spaced about a week apart, is the sweet spot. More than that risks annoyance for little gain.
- If you are using a LimeSurvey participant list, send reminders **only to people who haven't responded**. If you are using an open public link, you can't target non-responders, so phrase reminders as "if you haven't already…".

## What to say (the essentials)

Every message should make four things obvious:

1. **Why it matters.** You're shaping open source support at [ORGANIZATION].
2. **How long it takes.** About 15 to 20 minutes, less if you skip the optional section.
3. **It's anonymous.** "Not sure" is a fine answer.
4. **When it closes.** Give a clear deadline.

## Roles (small teams)

- **Owner.** Runs the campaign, owns the schedule, and sends the messages.
- **Sponsor** *(optional)*. A senior person who endorses it. Helpful for a wide campaign, not needed for a small, grassroots run.
- **Reviewer.** Your data-protection contact, who signs off the data-handling statement once.

## What to watch

- **Response count and rate** over the campaign. If reminders aren't moving the number, add a channel (a community post, a meeting mention) rather than another email.
- **Drop-off.** If many people start but don't finish, the survey may be landing at a bad time or feeling long. Consider trimming the optional personal-background section.

## Closing and sharing back

Close on the date you promised. After you've looked at the results, send a short summary of what you heard and what you plan to do. This is the single best thing you can do to make the *next* survey succeed, and it's why the survey's closing message invites people to email the survey contact to hear the outcome.
